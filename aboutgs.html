Sir William Gilbert (1836-1911) and Sir Arthur Sullivan (1842-1900) are perhaps the best known librettist and opera composer of Victorian England.

Sullivan, whose father was a musician and army bandmaster, studied music from the earliest age and became successful as a composer in a variety of classical styles. Gilbert went through a traditional English education and toyed with law jobs, but soon moved into comedy, and after a series of burlesque plays shifted towards more refined plays.

The first collaboration between Gilbert and Sullivan was in 1871 for the play Thespis. Later, in 1875, Richard D’Oyly Carte, impresario at London’s Royal Theatre, brought the two men together again to complete Trial by Jury, their first real success. Over the next twenty years, the two created twelve more light operas, including the most famous Pirates of Penzance (1879), H.M.S. Pinafore (1878), The Mikado (1885), and Iolanthe (1882). The shows were transferred to D’Oyly Carte’s Savoy Theater in 1881, which has become known ever since as the home of Gilbert and Sullivan works.

The shows are in general comedic satires on British society, often criticizing the nobility with dry musical wit (Iolanthe spends the whole show making fun of the House of Peers), or comparing British society to others (as in The Mikado, which is set in Japan). With one (and perhaps two) exception(s), no one dies in G+S shows and love always triumphs in the end – often with the help of some legalistic loophole found.

Both men were knighted (Sullivan in 1883, Gilbert in 1907), and both created numerous works on their own and with other collaborators, before, during, and after their partnership.
